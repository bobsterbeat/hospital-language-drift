# Methods Summary — Hospital Language Drift Study

## Suitable for Substack

We built a corpus of 683 press releases (~600,000 tokens) from five US academic medical centres — UC Davis Health, UCSF, Stanford Health Care/Stanford Medicine, Duke Health, and Michigan Medicine — spanning 2010 to 2025. Documents were discovered through the Wayback Machine CDX API (for historical coverage back to 2010), live newsroom pagination, and a small set of manually seeded strategic plans and annual reports. Each document was binned to its true publication year using, in priority order: a year in the URL slug (e.g. `/news/2011/04/...`), the `<meta property="article:published_time">` tag in the HTML head, or the Wayback crawl date as a fallback. (An earlier version of this analysis used the crawl date as the primary year, which incorrectly attributed many 2007–2011 articles to 2014–2016 because those snapshots happened to be crawled later; see Limitations.) HTML was extracted with trafilatura for boilerplate stripping with a minimum-length floor of 500 tokens; PDF documents were processed with PyMuPDF.

We measured two vocabulary categories against each document: an *operational* dictionary of 24 terms (utilisation, workflow, dashboard, scalable, alignment, deliverable, operational excellence, etc.) and a *relational* dictionary of 18 terms (compassion, bedside, physician, trust, dignity, suffering, empathy, kindness, etc.). Multi-word terms were matched by regex against raw lowercased text; unigrams were lemmatised with spaCy `en_core_web_sm`. Rates were normalised per 10,000 tokens to control for document length.

A linear mixed-effects model with system as a random intercept (statsmodels REML, n=1,366 observations across 5 system groups) found a year × category interaction of **+3.82 per year** (SE = 0.75, z = 5.09, p < 0.001): every calendar year, the operational-to-relational gap widens by ~3.8 units per 10,000 tokens. The relational rate alone declines at ~3.6 units/year (p < 0.001). To test whether the drift reflects healthcare-specific change rather than general shifts in written English, we pulled Google Books Ngrams rates (2010–2019) for each unigram in the two dictionaries and computed the ratio of healthcare corpus rate to general English rate; the relative depression of relational vocabulary in the medical corpus is preserved against this baseline.

Per-system rates per 10k tokens, early (2010–2016) vs late (2017–2025): UCSF 206 → 69 (−67%, n=4 early/420 late), Stanford 168 → 79 (−53%, 66/28), Michigan 88 → 38 (−57%, 7/6). UCDavis and Duke moved in the opposite direction in the per-cell averages (UCDavis 118 → 158, Duke 45 → 88), but their late-period cells contain only 6 documents each and the point estimates are correspondingly noisy.

Collocation analysis (±5 token windows, spaCy lemmas, pooled across systems) shows that "nurse" has shifted from an early context of *allied health professionals* (top co-occurrence) to *nurse practitioner* in the late period — suggesting the title is being used to describe an expanded scope-of-practice role rather than bedside presence. "Care" appears most often near *health*, *stanford*, and *patient* in the early period vs *patient*, *primary*, and *provide* in the late period.

**Limitations**: corpus collection from Wayback was hindered by intermittent `Connection refused` errors (final fetch success rate ~50% after retries), and the resulting per-cell sample sizes are severely uneven (UCSF 420 late vs 4 early; UCDavis/Duke 60–80 early vs 6 late). The mixed-effects model handles this with a random intercept for system and per-document residuals, but per-system point estimates carry wide confidence intervals for any cell with fewer than ~15 documents. Form 990 narrative sections — the document type with the strongest predicted operational signal — were blocked at download by ProPublica's access controls and are absent from this run. Strategic plans were too sparse (n=3) for system-level comparison. Replication with a larger and more balanced corpus is warranted before treating any per-system claim as settled; the cross-system mixed-effects interaction is the most robust finding.

*Corpus: n = 683 documents, ~600,000 tokens. Code and data: https://github.com/bobsterbeat/hospital-language-drift*
